Traveling with zl (Part Four) - Reunion After a Long Separation

When the oppressive days had crawled past over seven hundred cycles on the calendar, and a glimmer of light finally appeared in my work and life, I decided to end this long self-exile. I planned to travel across the ocean at the end of September to visit ZL, whom I hadn't seen in three full years.

I don't know when it started, but the starting point of my travels always seems to be after the journey begins. That is, there's no week-long anticipation before departure, no nightly eyes-closed daydreaming about the wonderful things that might happen on this trip—just packing and leaving. And I was still solving work problems right before heading out. This made the whole process too rational and lacking in emotion. This continued until I ordered a small bottle of wine on the plane. As it went down, a warmth rose from my chest, driving away that excess rationality. Only then did my anticipation for the unknown and my nervousness about reuniting with an old friend start dancing in my mind.

After getting off the plane and exiting the station, I spotted him. He had cut his hair short and was no longer skinny. Now he was a sturdy man with a perfect haircut. Seeing each other, as we approached, neither of us could suppress our smiling lips; the old flame reignited. The outdoor air was a bit crisp, and we climbed into his small car.

Soon, after the joy of reunion settled, I noticed—maybe because it had been so long, or perhaps it was a necessity of North American life—that he seemed to have armored himself with a transparent shell, blocking out me and everything unknown in the world. What he showed the world was an emotionally stable, robust man. Yet in certain moments, I could glimpse the immature youth beneath that shell.

Luckily, that shell wasn't stable. In zl's little car, the passing scenery soon crushed it under the force of time. After 36 hours, we were back to chatting about everything under the sun, just like in those years in Suzhou, even shamelessly commenting on every girl who walked by—except for Asian faces.

Among the many dreams that dissolved into water and were forgotten in the rivers and lakes, one was to drive on PCH while listening to Jaden's song "PCH". Before arriving in Monterey, I mentioned this to ZL. Without a word, he switched to that song and put it on a loop. Under the sunny sky, by the foggy and blue sea, as the intro of PCH filled the air, the sea breeze took shape. We chased the ever-shrinking horizon, and the rolling wheels kept grinding out new memories to patch and cover my lost dreams.

Sometimes the setting sun was ahead of us. I stared at the sunset in silence, worn out by jet lag. ZL, who was driving at the time, was also silent. Twilight mixed with the streetlights painted our faces. We drove toward the immortal sun under the watch of the evening gods, and in that silence was an endless conversation. Then he broke the silence: "Tsk, we need a song that fits this moment." ZL said while fiddling with the main screen.

Traveling with zl (Part Three) - How about cycling around Qinghai Lake together?

Summer 2021, a few weeks before zl went off to school, we arranged to cycle around Qinghai Lake. A six-day, five-night trip, with four full days of cycling around the lake: 360 km total, averaging 90 km per day. A week after returning, even with normal eating, I still lost 5 pounds.

This kind of crazy travel plan—I'd been wanting to do it again ever since my first attempt before college in the summer of 2014. Over the years, I've been urging every friend around me, but zl was the only one who showed great interest and actively responded.

We started from the bike rental shop in Haiyan County (I brought my own bike; zl rented a touring bike) and cycled clockwise around the lake.

The sky and sea merged into one line under a vast clear sky. As far as the eye could see, there was only a road stretching to the horizon. Under the expansive blue sky was an even bluer sea, beside which were large fields of rapeseed flowers. On the road beside those fields were only the two of us.

For four days, the world was compressed into rhythmic pedaling—left, right. Heaven and earth contained only us. Our tired bodies supported our endlessly active minds. We didn't even have time to listen to music; our conversation was hard to end.

Over seven years, Qinghai Lake had been expanding. Statues that stood on the shore in 2014 were half-submerged in water by 2021. I wondered if, within my lifetime, Qinghai Lake would reconnect with rivers and gradually become a freshwater lake. And if, by then, we would come again—not daring to hope for a full lake loop, but at least to drink a big pot of milk tea in a tent, and boast about how interesting it all was.

The only things that added a little bitterness to the trip were: the severe migraines from altitude sickness that lasted almost half the journey, several 30-kilometer uphill stretches, occasional torrential rain, poor accommodation, and the particularly tangled spirit of a young twenty-something.

Worth noting: the last day's ride came with continuous rain and endless uphill climbs. Out of our 24 Snickers bars, only 6 were left. Red Bull was getting nauseating. By afternoon, I vaguely felt the root of my tongue growing cold—a bad sign. During a break 20 km from the finish, zl's sense of humor was still firing, producing a masterpiece: a photo of c1 peeing. Fortunately, a Tibetan truck driver asked if we needed help. The moment I sat in the warm, dry cab with the AC on, I realized just how cold it had been. I shivered for another ten minutes on the road.

Back in Xining, we devoured two jin of hand-grabbed lamb. That was the last time I saw zl before autumn of this year.

Tiefling sorcerer Phil Cavendish had an unusual Midwinter Festival.

Three months ago, to cheer up his depressed mother, he packed his bags and set off for Waterdeep to find his irresponsible older sister and convince her to come home soon—Alice Cavendish.

As an adopted child, Phil had learned early on, during his years as a street performer, that some people just don't appreciate what they have. Here she was, throwing away a perfectly good noble lady's life to go off and explore.

But it was precisely this willful sister's insistence that had given the orphan Phil a warm and comfortable home over a decade ago. Phil's feelings for his sister were complicated.

However, after nearly a month of searching, Phil's patience was wearing thin. Thinking about it, anger toward his sister took over. And not only that—she had even chosen to follow Sune. The more Phil thought about it, the angrier he got. Without realizing it, he triggered a Wild Magic Surge, and the chaotic power in his blood teleported him right in front of the sister he had just been mentally scolding.
