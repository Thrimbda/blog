Traveling with ZL (Part Four) - Reunion After a Long Time

When over seven hundred days of oppressive time had crawled across the calendar, and a faint glimmer finally appeared in my work life, I decided to end this long self-exile. I planned to cross the ocean at the end of September to visit ZL, whom I hadn't seen for three whole years.

I don't know when it started, but the starting point of my travels always seems to be after the journey has begun. That is to say, there's no anticipation lasting one or two weeks before departure, no lying in bed at night imagining the wonderful things that will happen on this trip. It's just packing and leaving, and right before departure, I was still solving work problems. This made the whole process too rational, lacking emotion. This continued until I asked for a small bottle of wine on the plane. The warmth rising from my chest after drinking it dispelled this excess rationality. Only then did my anticipation for the unknown and my nervousness about reuniting with an old friend I hadn't seen in so long begin to dance in my mind.

After getting off the plane and exiting the station, I spotted him. He had cut his hair short and was no longer skinny. Now he was a burly guy with just the right hair length. Seeing each other, we couldn't suppress our rising smiles as we gradually approached. The old flame was rekindled. The outdoor air was a bit crisp, and we ducked into his little car.
Soon, after the joy of reunion subsided, I noticed‚Äîwhether it was because we hadn't seen each other for so long or because it was necessary for life in North America‚Äîthat he seemed to have armed himself with a transparent shell, keeping me and all the world's unknowns at bay. What he showed the world was an emotionally stable, burly man, but in certain moments, you could glimpse the awkward youth beneath that shell.
Fortunately, it was an unstable shell. In ZL's little car, the scenery along the way quickly crushed the shell under the influence of time. Within 36 hours, we were once again chatting and joking like we did during those years in Suzhou, and shamelessly commenting on every girl who walked past us‚Äîexcept for Asian faces.

Among my many dreams of dissolving into the river and being forgotten by the world, one was to drive on the PCH while listening to Jaden's song "PCH." Before reaching Monterey, I mentioned this to ZL. Without a word, he found the song and put it on repeat. Under the clear sunlight, by the misty, azure sea, as the intro to "PCH" flowed out, the sea breeze took shape. We chased the constantly collapsing horizon, the rolling wheels continuously grinding out new memories to patch over my lost dreams.

Sometimes the sunset was ahead of us. Exhausted from jet lag, I stared at the evening glow in silence while ZL, who was driving, was also silent. Twilight and streetlights fell on our faces. Under the gaze of the twilight gods, we drove toward the immortal sun, and within this silence was an uninterrupted conversation. Then he broke the silence: "Tsk, need to find a song suitable for this moment," ZL said as he fiddled with the main control screen.

Traveling with ZL (Part Three) - Want to Go Cycling Around Qinghai Lake?

In the summer of 2021, a few weeks before ZL went abroad to study, we agreed to go cycling around Qinghai Lake. The trip was six days and five nights, with the actual cycling around the lake taking four full days: a total of 360 km, averaging 90 km per day. I lost 5 pounds in the week after returning, even after my diet returned to normal.

I had wanted to do another crazy travel plan like this ever since implementing one during the summer before starting university in 2014. Over the years, I had urged and called upon every friend around me. ZL was the only one who showed great interest and responded enthusiastically.

We set off from a bike rental shop in Haiyan County (I brought my own bike, ZL rented a touring bike) and cycled clockwise around the lake.

The sky and water merged into one line under a clear, vast sky. Looking out, there was only the road stretching to the horizon. Under the expansive blue sky was an even bluer sea, next to which were large fields of rapeseed. On the road beside the fields, there was only the two of us.
For four days, the world was compressed into the rhythmic pedaling of left and right. Between heaven and earth, there were only the two of us. Our tired bodies supported infinitely energetic minds. We didn't even bother listening to music; our conversations were hard to end.

Qinghai Lake has been expanding for seven years. A statue that stood on the shore in 2014 was half submerged in water by 2021. I wondered if, in my lifetime, Qinghai Lake would reconnect with rivers and gradually become a freshwater lake again. Would we come back then? Not daring to hope for another cycling trip, but at least to drink a large pot of milk tea in a tent. And then brag about how fun it was.

Only the severe migraines from altitude sickness that lasted almost half the journey, several continuous 30 km uphill climbs, occasional heavy rain, poor accommodation conditions, and the particular awkwardness of a 24-25-year-old artsy youth could add a little bitterness to this trip.

It's worth mentioning that the last day of cycling was accompanied by continuous heavy rain and endless uphill climbs. Our 24 Snickers bars were down to the last 6, and we were almost sick of drinking Red Bull. In the afternoon, I vaguely felt a chill at the base of my tongue and realized this was a bad sign. While resting 20 km from the finish line, ZL's sense of humor was still outputting, leaving behind the masterpiece "c1 Peeing Picture." Fortunately, a Tibetan truck driver asked if we needed help. The moment I sat in the warm, dry, air-conditioned cab, I realized how cold I was. I shivered for another ten minutes on the road.

After returning to Xining, we fiercely ate two pounds of hand-grabbed mutton. That was the last time I saw ZL before this autumn.

Life in Suzhou was like university. We were in the High-speed Rail New Town in the far north of Suzhou, surrounded by nothing. By 9 PM every night, the streets were quiet everywhere, and it was hard to even see a person.
There were very few rental options near the company. Most people chose the same apartment building, a ten-minute walk from the office. This was the "dormitory" of Momenta University.
